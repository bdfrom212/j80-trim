J/80 / Trim

The backstay is your throttle. The 4:1 split backstay bends the mast, straightens the forestay, and flattens the main — North Sails calls it the most dynamic trim on the boat. Sheet sets twist; traveler sets angle.

BackstayPrimary depower — bends mast, straightens forestay, flattens main. Key in all winds.
SheetSets twist — read off the top-leech telltale (it stalls first).
TravelerSets angle of attack — increase by falling off or raising traveler. Read luff telltales. Use traveler (not sheet) to adjust angle without changing sail shape.
Off-windEase backstay for power; vang takes over leech/twist.

Trim from scratch · any point of sail

1Backstay — pick your gear for the wind.
2Sheet — set twist to the top-leech telltale.
3Traveler — set angle to the luff telltales & helm.
4Cunningham + outhaul — fine-tune draft & foot.
5Jib to the slot, then re-check the main twist.

Big controls first, fine controls last — then iterate.

Building breeze → depower · in order

1Backstay on — bend mast, flatten main, firm forestay.
2Cunningham on — draft forward as mast bends.
3Outhaul on — flat foot.
4Traveler down — shed heel & helm.
5Vang on, ease sheet in puffs — vang-sheet.
6Jib — halyard up, lead aft.

Shifting up through the gears as it builds.

Lulls → power up · reverse it

1Ease backstay — mast straightens, main deepens.
2Ease cunningham & outhaul — restore depth.
3Traveler up — angle back on.
4Sheet on — trim to the telltale.
5Jib — halyard eased, lead forward.

Rig base · at the dock

1Mast butt → ~3.5–3.75″ prebend.
2Uppers to base — mast in column.
3Lowers, then intermediates — still in column.
4Rake at base tension, backstay fully eased.
5Match backstay turnbuckles to the shrouds.
6Leave the dock at base (6–10 kn); trim from there.

The zipper — how the leech stalls

Sheet in = leech closes top-first, like a zipper. The top telltale stalls first — that's your warning. The lower telltales stall last — that's already too far. Trim to "just catching the top tooth."
Too eased
All flying — speed on the table
Right
Top just stalling — rest flying
Over-trimmed
Zipper stuck — ease sheet
●If the bottom stalls while the top flies freely — it's not the sheet. It's the slot: the jib's too tight. Check jib trim first.

Jib luff telltales · steering

●Both streaming aft = trimmed right. Windward lifts → too high or eased (bear off / trim). Leeward droops limp → stalled (ease / come up). Never let the leeward one die.
●Heavy air: let the upper windward telltale stop flowing to depower the top. Feather up so they lift in the puffs.

Angle of attack & the groove

●Low groove — both telltales streaming straight aft = attached flow both sides. Most lift.
●Middle groove — windward telltales lifting 45° to vertical = edge of stalling. Best lift-to-drag ratio. Most efficient.
●High groove — windward side stalling more frequently. Temporary higher pointing but less power. Main starts to soften at mast.
●Leeward telltales stall just below the groove — especially helpful in light air when other indicators are less reliable. Bear off to just above the stall point.
●Puff: increases apparent wind and angle of attack. Ease sail or drop traveller to compensate. Leeward telltales may stall.
●Lull: decreases angle of attack. May need to bear off if it persists.
●Twist creates a smaller angle of attack at top of sail. Reduce twist to increase pointing — squeeze top telltale to stall 50–75% of the time.
○Add mainsail twist in heavy waves to compensate for pitching.

Jib lead · the car rule

●"Move the car toward the unhappy telltale." Luff up slowly and watch the jib's windward telltales. Top breaks first → too much twist / leech too open → move car forward. Bottom breaks first → leech too tight, open for twist → move car aft. Goal: both break together.
●Leeward telltale drooping = sheet issue, not a lead issue.
●Mark the car for light / medium / heavy and you've nailed 90% of it.
○Re-tuning the lead for every shift and gust.

Main leech telltales · all heights

●Top batten — your trim setpoint (stalls first = early warning).
Light/chopAll telltales flying ~100% — more twist, keep flow attached.
Powered/flatTop stalls occasionally. Mid and low still flying — confirm the power section is attached.
HeavyBackstay twists top open — all flying freely again (depower, not under-trim).
●Mid/lower telltales — confirm the powerful section is attached. If one stalls while the top flies: that's the slot. Check the jib, not the sheet.
○Add mainsail twist in heavy waves to compensate for pitching.

Reaching & running

●Main: mid-leech telltale becomes the key reference — keep it streaming. Accept the top lifting a little and the bottom hanging; that's the twist compromise on a reach.
●Jib: ease to the luff, then trim back on; lead forward & outboard.
●Kite: ease to the luff curl, trim a hair, play it constantly.
○Dead run: main is stalled — trust the angle, telltales won't fly reliably.

Under ~5 kn · telltales won't fly

●Heel to leeward — gravity pulls the sails into shape.
●Trim by eye — deep, twisted, eased; protect the shape once set.
●Masthead fly + read the water — steer to pressure and the fly, not the dead yarn.
●Speed & feel — minimal rudder, crew dead still, foot off to build way.

Puff direction · header, lift, or neutral

Approach
Response
Forward of 45° (ahead of beam)
Header. Think tack immediately as the puff hits. Every meter sailed in a header is distance lost to windward — tacking also shrinks your tack angle (e.g. a 20° header cuts a 90° tack to 70°).
45°–60° (abeam zone)
Neutral — probably in line with the wind you're sailing in. Ease/feather, hike, and trim through it.
Aft of 60° (behind beam)
Lift. Ease and smoothly head up to the new higher course while trimming in — don't slam the trim, ease into it.

Watch the puff's approach angle on the water before it hits — that tells you what kind of shift it's carrying.

The upwind groove spectrum

●Live at the Twitchy Luff line — the boundary between On Angle and Height — for best VMG. Windward telltale dances/lifts intermittently, leeward stays pinned.
●Drop to Low/Fast after a tack, in chop, or coming out of a lull to rebuild speed.
●Push to Height only when you need it — fetching a mark, crossing a boat. It's a temporary trade of speed for angle, not a resting state.
○Pinching loses both speed and height — almost never the right call.
